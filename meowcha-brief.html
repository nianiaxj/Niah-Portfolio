Why Meowcha works.

Not just what it looks like. What it understood about the people it was made for.

Brand Audience

The burnout crowd.

Millennials and Gen Z in cities. They scroll slow-living accounts between back-to-back meetings and spend Sunday dreading Monday. They don't need more fun. They need somewhere that asks nothing of them. No performance. No output. Just time that belongs to them.

Brand Objective

A safe space to just be.

Not a café that happens to have cats. A community built around rest and presence. Meowcha is where the burnout crowd exhales. The whole brand — colors, fonts, tone — does the same job: telling the right person it's okay to stop here.

What we said no to, and why.

Sharp sans-serifs (Inter, Helvetica)
They're the fonts of apps, decks, dashboards. One glance and it reads "work." That's the exact feeling Meowcha exists to undo.
Pure white backgrounds (#FFFFFF)
Clinical, bright, office-screen energy. White is what people stare at all week. The palette had to feel nothing like that.
Cold grays and blues
They read like Slack, Notion, every productivity tool in the world. The opposite of what this brand is for.
Rigid geometric layouts
Hard corners, grid-perfect structure — feels like a pitch deck. A place that wants you to slow down shouldn't look like it's trying to impress someone.

Typography Rationale

Chewy for display. The rounded, nibbly letterforms were chosen because they remind you of a chubby cat's tail. That softness is not childish. It's deliberately not trying to be sharp or impressive. Sharp means corporate. Meowcha is the opposite.

Lora for body. A serif that reads warm, not formal. Academic serifs feel like textbooks. Lora feels like a note someone actually wrote. Together the two fonts say: this brand has character, and it's not in a hurry.

Core Insight
People don't follow cafes. They follow feelings they want more of. Every Meowcha post leads with that feeling first, product second. The content doesn't look like marketing because it's not trying to sell anything. It's giving people permission to rest. That's what makes them come back.
