brand strategy

Why Meocha works. (Or would.)

Audience

Gen-Z and millennial women, mid-20s, city-based. They're in a 9-to-5 (or they've just left one). They want their weekends to feel like theirs again. Not productive, not curated for social media, just slow. They follow accounts that give them permission to rest. Meocha is for them.

Brand Objective

Not to sell matcha. To sell the feeling of a Sunday where nothing is required of you. Meocha is a place (physical or digital) where the burnout crowd goes to exhale. The brand's job is to feel like that before they even walk in the door.

Color Rationale

Muted sage Nature that nurtures. Not the trendy matcha green. The quiet kind. The color of a plant you've kept alive. It says: you're safe to put your phone down.
Warm cream Unhurried. A Sunday morning. A page nothing's been written on yet.
Soft terracotta Grounded, human, a little earthy. Keeps the palette from feeling too precious or fragile.
Blush pink (accent) A cat's inner ear. That specific pink. It's an attention-to-detail choice. If you notice it, you're paying exactly the right kind of attention. That's the Meocha customer.

Typography

The fonts are round-edged and slightly nibbly. The specific reference: a chubby cat's tail. That softness isn't childish. It's deliberately not trying to be sharp or impressive. In a world of sans-serifs performing competence, Meocha's type performs comfort. Every font choice is a brand decision, not a style preference.

Content Strategy

The 2-week calendar was mapped to day-of-week mood, not just a posting cadence. Weekend posts: soft, atmospheric, no call to action. Weekday posts: acknowledge the grind without amplifying it. The brand never performs positivity. It holds space. That distinction is what makes a content strategy, not just a content schedule.

What I rejected

The ultra-minimal Japanese-inspired matcha aesthetic that's everywhere right now. Clean whites, sparse layouts, wellness-product energy. Meocha isn't selling a health product. It's selling a place to exhale. Those are different briefs and they need different visual systems.

Content Calendar

14 posts across two weeks, mapped to day-of-week mood rather than just a posting schedule. Weekends leaned soft and atmospheric. Weekdays acknowledged the grind without amplifying it. Each post was assigned a content pillar, format, and tone note before a single caption was written.

CRUMBS (short film)

A film about a woman who's exhausted. Not loudly, just completely. Warm tones gone stale, heavy grain, a composition that feels a little tired on purpose.

I WILL DREAM (poster)

About nostalgia and fragmentation: the feeling of a memory you can't quite hold. The font is bold and deliberate, an angry exploration of longing. Red for that edge: nostalgia isn't always soft.

Live Project Game Design Interactive

Lavender's Run

Lavender is done. She quits. This is a Pac-Man riff built from a brief I gave myself: what if a classic arcade mechanic was about burnout instead of dots? Lavender is a hand-drawn original character (designed, named, and animated by me) navigating an office she's trying to leave. The collectibles are unread emails. The start button says "submit resignation."

The concept, the copy, the character, and the code are all mine. CRUMBS, Meocha, this game. They keep asking the same question: what does it feel like when someone needs out?

Live Project Data Viz UI Design

INSGHT: Social Telemetry

What if Instagram analytics felt like checking your blog stats in 2003? INSGHT is a real data dashboard dressed in an early-2000s internet skin. Pink on dark. Monospaced type. A scrolling ticker. Your actual numbers (views, reach, profile visits, engagement rate), just wearing something better.

The same data looks different depending on how it's framed. The design is the argument.
